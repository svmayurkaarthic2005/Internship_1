SURVEY NUMBER AND SUB-DIVISION MANUAL FOR SIS OFFICERS

=== SURVEY NUMBER NAMING CONVENTION ===

Survey Number Structure:
- Survey numbers are unique identifiers for land parcels within a block
- Format: Numeric (e.g., 145, 267, 1023) or Alphanumeric (e.g., 145A, 267B)
- Each survey number belongs to exactly one block
- Survey numbers cannot be duplicated within the same block
- Survey numbers are permanent and do not change unless merged

Survey Number Components:
- Base Number: Primary identifier (e.g., 145)
- Optional Suffix: Letter suffix for related parcels (e.g., 145A, 145B)
- Block Association: Each survey number is linked to a specific block
- Ward Association: Block is part of a ward
- Town Association: Ward is part of a town

Example Hierarchy:
District: Chennai
  ↳ Taluk: Mylapore
    ↳ Town: T. Nagar
      ↳ Ward: Ward 5
        ↳ Block: Block 12
          ↳ Survey Numbers: 145, 146, 147, 148A, 148B

=== SUB-DIVISION NUMBERING RULES ===

When to Create Sub-Division:
- When a single survey number is partitioned into multiple parcels
- When ownership of part of a survey number is transferred
- When land is sold in parts requiring separate patta
- When inheritance division requires separate land records

Sub-Division Numbering Pattern:
Original Survey: 145
First Level Sub-Division: 145/1, 145/2, 145/3
Second Level Sub-Division: 145/1A, 145/1B, 145/2A, 145/2B
Third Level Sub-Division: 145/1A1, 145/1A2, 145/1B1

Sub-Division Numbering Examples:

Example 1: Simple Partition
Survey 145 (1000 sq.m) divided into 2 parts
Result: 145/1 (600 sq.m), 145/2 (400 sq.m)

Example 2: Multiple Sub-Divisions
Survey 267 (2000 sq.m) divided into 4 parts
Result: 267/1 (500 sq.m), 267/2 (500 sq.m), 267/3 (600 sq.m), 267/4 (400 sq.m)

Example 3: Further Sub-Division
Survey 145/1 (600 sq.m) further divided into 2 parts
Result: 145/1A (350 sq.m), 145/1B (250 sq.m)

Example 4: Complex Sub-Division
Survey 89 → 89/1, 89/2, 89/3
Survey 89/1 → 89/1A, 89/1B
Survey 89/1A → 89/1A1, 89/1A2

Sub-Division Naming Rules:
1. Use sequential numbers for first-level sub-division (1, 2, 3...)
2. Use letters for second-level sub-division (A, B, C...)
3. Use numbers again for third-level sub-division (1, 2, 3...)
4. Always maintain parent reference in naming
5. Do not skip numbers or letters in sequence
6. Maximum sub-division depth: 3 levels (discouraged beyond this)

=== AREA VALIDATION RULES ===

Critical Rule: Sum of All Sub-Divisions Must Equal Original Survey Area

Example Validation:
Original Survey 145: 1000 sq.m
Sub-Divisions:
  - 145/1: 300 sq.m
  - 145/2: 250 sq.m
  - 145/3: 450 sq.m
  Total: 300 + 250 + 450 = 1000 sq.m ✓ VALID

Invalid Example:
Original Survey 267: 2000 sq.m
Sub-Divisions:
  - 267/1: 800 sq.m
  - 267/2: 900 sq.m
  Total: 800 + 900 = 1700 sq.m ✗ INVALID (Missing 300 sq.m)

Area Measurement Standards:
- All areas recorded in square meters (sq.m)
- Minimum measurable area: 1 sq.m
- Maximum precision: 2 decimal places (e.g., 456.75 sq.m)
- Survey measurements must be verified in field
- GPS coordinates mandatory for area verification

Area Calculation Tolerance:
- Allowed variance: ±0.5% for survey measurements
- Example: 1000 sq.m survey can have 995-1005 sq.m total
- Variance beyond tolerance requires re-survey
- DIS approval required for variance explanation

Common Area Validation Errors:
1. Missing Sub-Division: Not all parts of original survey accounted for
2. Overlapping Sub-Divisions: Same area claimed by multiple sub-divisions
3. Excess Area: Total sub-division area exceeds original survey
4. Measurement Error: Field measurement differs from document

=== ENCROACHMENT DETECTION PROCESS ===

What is Encroachment:
- Unauthorized occupation of survey land
- Construction beyond approved boundaries
- Encroachment by neighboring survey numbers
- Public land or road encroachment onto private survey
- Private survey encroachment onto public land/road

Types of Encroachment:

1. Boundary Encroachment
   - Building or fence extends beyond survey boundary
   - Detected during field visit with GPS measurement
   - Requires boundary stone verification

2. Full Encroachment
   - Entire survey number occupied by unauthorized person
   - Different person than patta holder
   - Common in abandoned or disputed lands

3. Partial Encroachment
   - Part of survey number encroached
   - Common in agricultural lands
   - Detected through area measurement mismatch

4. Public Space Encroachment
   - Survey land encroaching onto road or public path
   - Requires clearance from local body
   - May require demolition or regularization

Encroachment Detection Steps:

Step 1: Document Review
- Check survey sketch and boundary descriptions
- Review previous field visit reports
- Check for litigation history

Step 2: Physical Verification
- Visit survey location with GPS device
- Mark survey corners using GPS coordinates
- Measure actual boundaries

Step 3: Boundary Stone Check
- Locate boundary stones (if installed)
- Verify boundary stone numbers
- Check for moved or missing boundary stones

Step 4: Neighbor Verification
- Check neighboring survey boundaries
- Identify common boundary points
- Get neighbor acknowledgment if possible

Step 5: Documentation
- Take photographs from all angles
- Record GPS coordinates of all corners
- Measure encroached area
- Identify encroaching party (if known)

Step 6: Report Preparation
- Prepare encroachment report with evidence
- Include sketch showing encroachment
- Recommend action: removal, regularization, or legal action

Encroachment Flag in Application:
- If encroachment detected, application is flagged
- Flag prevents automatic approval
- Requires DIS and Tahsildar review
- May require legal opinion
- Timeline extends by 15-30 days for resolution

=== LITIGATION FLAG PROCESS ===

What is Litigation:
- Ongoing court case related to survey land
- Ownership dispute between multiple parties
- Boundary dispute with neighboring survey
- Stay order from civil or revenue court
- Criminal case involving land fraud or forgery

Types of Litigation:

1. Title Dispute
   - Multiple parties claiming ownership
   - Inherited property dispute
   - Will contest
   - Adverse possession claim

2. Boundary Dispute
   - Disagreement on survey boundaries
   - Conflicting survey documents
   - Encroachment dispute with neighbor

3. Revenue Dispute
   - Property tax assessment dispute
   - Land classification dispute (agricultural vs. residential)
   - Revenue recovery proceedings

4. Criminal Litigation
   - Land grab cases
   - Document forgery
   - Fraudulent sale deed
   - Impersonation

Litigation Detection Process:

Step 1: EC Verification
- Check Encumbrance Certificate for litigation remarks
- Look for court case reference numbers
- Check for stay orders or injunctions

Step 2: Revenue Records Check
- Verify if litigation flag exists in revenue system
- Check Tahsildar remarks
- Review previous application history

Step 3: Field Inquiry
- Ask current occupant about disputes
- Check with neighbors about any known disputes
- Verify if property is locked or abandoned (indicator of dispute)

Step 4: Court Records Check
- Cross-check with civil court case database
- Check revenue court records
- Verify if stay order is active

Litigation Flag Actions:

If Litigation Detected:
1. Mark application with litigation flag
2. Record litigation details: court name, case number, parties involved
3. Application status changed to "On Hold - Litigation"
4. Notify applicant about litigation issue
5. Recommend legal consultation
6. Application can proceed only after:
   - Court stay is vacated
   - Court judgment in applicant's favor
   - Settlement agreement between parties

Litigation Reference Format:
- Court Name: "Madras High Court"
- Case Number: "O.S. 145/2023"
- Case Type: "Title Suit"
- Status: "Pending" or "Disposed"
- Stay Order: "Yes/No"

Timeline Impact:
- Applications with litigation are kept on hold
- No timeline applies during litigation hold
- Application resumes after litigation clearance
- Fresh field visit may be required after clearance

=== MERGE RULES: WHICH SUB-DIVISIONS CAN BE MERGED ===

Survey Number Merging:
- Combines multiple survey numbers or sub-divisions into single survey number
- Used when adjacent land parcels are purchased by same owner
- Simplifies land records and patta management

Merge Eligibility Criteria:

1. Common Ownership
   - All survey numbers must have same owner
   - Ownership must be sole (not joint) or all joint owners must agree
   - Patta holder name must match across all surveys

2. Geographic Adjacency
   - Survey numbers must share common boundary
   - Non-adjacent surveys cannot be merged
   - At least one common boundary point required

3. Same Jurisdiction
   - All surveys must belong to same block
   - Surveys from different blocks cannot be merged
   - Surveys must be in same ward and town

4. Same Land Classification
   - All surveys must have same land type (agricultural, residential, commercial)
   - Mixed land types cannot be merged
   - Requires reclassification before merging

5. No Encumbrance
   - No mortgage, loan, or charge on any survey
   - No litigation pending on any survey
   - No encroachment on any survey

6. Clear Title
   - Encumbrance Certificate must be clear for all surveys
   - No disputes or claims on any survey
   - Revenue arrears must be cleared

Merge Process:

Step 1: Merge Application Submission
- Application type: MERGE
- List all survey numbers to be merged
- Provide ownership proof for all surveys
- Submit EC for all surveys

Step 2: SIS Officer Verification
- Verify common ownership
- Check physical adjacency in field
- Verify all surveys belong to same block
- Check for any encumbrance or litigation
- Prepare merge sketch showing combined area

Step 3: Area Calculation
- Calculate total area of merged survey
- New Survey Area = Sum of all individual survey areas
- Example: Survey 145 (500 sq.m) + Survey 146 (600 sq.m) = Merged Survey 145M (1100 sq.m)

Step 4: New Survey Number Assignment
- Merged survey gets new survey number
- Usually based on lowest original survey number with 'M' suffix
- Example: 145 + 146 + 147 → 145M
- Alternative: Retain lowest number, mark others as "merged into 145"

Step 5: DIS Approval
- DIS reviews merge application
- Verifies compliance with merge rules
- Approves new survey number and area

Step 6: Revenue Records Update
- Old survey numbers marked as "Merged"
- New survey number created in system
- Single patta issued for merged survey
- Property tax records consolidated

Sub-Division Merge Examples:

Example 1: Simple Sub-Division Merge
Sub-Divisions: 145/1 (300 sq.m) + 145/2 (400 sq.m)
Result: Reverts to Survey 145 (700 sq.m)
Sub-divisions 145/1 and 145/2 marked as "Merged back to parent"

Example 2: Multiple Sub-Division Merge
Sub-Divisions: 267/1A (200 sq.m) + 267/1B (250 sq.m)
Result: Reverts to Survey 267/1 (450 sq.m)
Sub-divisions 267/1A and 267/1B marked as "Merged"

Example 3: Cross-Survey Merge Not Allowed
Sub-Divisions: 145/1 (300 sq.m) + 267/1 (400 sq.m)
Result: NOT ALLOWED - Different parent survey numbers
Recommendation: First merge back to parent, then merge parent surveys

Merge Restrictions:

Cannot Merge:
1. Surveys with different owners
2. Non-adjacent surveys
3. Surveys from different blocks
4. Surveys with pending litigation
5. Surveys with active mortgages
6. Surveys with different land classification
7. Surveys with encroachment issues
8. Surveys where one has sub-divisions still active

Timeline for Merge Application:
- SIS Verification: 10 working days
- Field Visit: Mandatory within 15 days
- DIS Approval: 5 working days
- Revenue Update: 3 working days
- Total: Approximately 25-30 working days
